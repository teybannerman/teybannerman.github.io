Most people in AI come from one world. Engineers who think in systems. Designers who think in experiences. Business leaders who think in outcomes. I've spent serious time in all three, and that's shaped how I see problems — and how I help others see them differently.

I started writing code at 14. Studied Computer Science, then worked as a software developer. Then made the unusual decision to walk away from engineering and pursue design — a transition that confused almost everyone in the 2000s. Spent a decade building digital products, leading teams, and learning that the gap between technology that works and technology people actually want to use is almost always a human problem, not a technical one.

That conviction led me to McKinsey & Company, where I led the European design practice and pioneered some of the firm's earliest generative AI work with clients. Over eight years, I rose to Partner.

Along the way, I built AI-powered advisory platforms for banks, led digital transformations spanning 16 countries, created personalisation engines for global brands, and kept coming back to the same lesson: the organisations that succeed with AI are the ones that start with how people actually make decisions, not with what the technology can theoretically do.

Today, I work independently — advising, building, teaching, and writing about the practical realities of making AI work within human systems. I advise companies and leaders on AI strategy and human-centred AI implementation. I'm lead lecturer and course creator for iF Academy's AI strategy programme for design leaders, a lecturer on D&AD's AI course, and faculty at the Marketing Academy. I co-authored a book that reached #1 on Amazon's global AI charts. And I've developed practical tools — like the Four Modes and the cultural intelligence map — now used by thousands of practitioners worldwide.

But the tools are expressions of something deeper: a way of thinking about technology that centres human judgment, respects cultural complexity, and insists on practical clarity over impressive abstraction. That's what I bring to advisory relationships. And it's what drives the question I keep returning to: how do we build technology that genuinely serves people?

When I'm not advising, I'm building. [The Purposeful AI Project](/purposeful-ai-project/) catalogues AI that's creating meaningful positive change in the world. [Tiny Lab of Wonders](/tiny-lab/) is where I make small tools and experiments that help people or bring joy — a space to stay curious, stay honest about what technology actually feels like from the inside, and keep my hands in the work.

## What I believe

<ul class="post-list">
	<li>
		<h4>Every failed AI initiative I've studied started with the wrong question.</h4>
They asked "what can this technology do?" instead of "what decision is this person actually trying to make?" Starting with the human context changes everything — the architecture, the interface, the governance, the adoption.
	</li>
	<li>
		<h4>The most important AI skill isn't technical.</h4>
It's the ability to see your own thinking clearly enough to know where a machine can genuinely help. That's why the Four Modes resonates with so many people — it's not really about AI. It's about understanding how you work.
	</li>
	<li>
		<h4>Cultural context isn't a nice-to-have in AI deployment.</h4>
An AI system trained predominantly on English-language data will embed assumptions about communication, trust, and respect that fail silently in other cultures. I've seen this derail global rollouts that were technically flawless.
	</li>
	<li>
		<h4>Ethics is an engineering problem, not a compliance exercise.</h4>
Responsible AI requires building trust calibration systems, testing for cultural intelligence, and designing governance that enables progress rather than creating bureaucratic theatre.
	</li>
	<li>
		<h4>Complexity is easy. Clarity is hard. And worth it.</h4>
The most impactful work I've done has always involved making something simpler, not more sophisticated. The best idea is the one people actually use on Monday morning.
	</li>
	<li>
		<h4>The goal isn't AI adoption. The goal is better decisions.</h4>
Technology is the enabler, not the destination. The organisations that understand this will build AI systems people trust. The rest will build expensive tools that sit unused.
	</li>
</ul>
